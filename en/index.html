What a browser fingerprint is made of

A site needs no cookie: from a handful of seemingly harmless questions your browser answers, it can assemble a fairly stable identifier. Common signals include subtle GPU and driver differences when rendering canvas/WebGL, your installed font list, timezone, screen resolution and colour depth, languages, CPU cores, device memory and touch support. No single value is unique, but combined they usually are.

How identifying each signal is (entropy)

Entropy measures how much identifying information a signal carries (in bits - higher means it separates people better). EFF's Panopticlick measured about 18.1 bits on average, with roughly 83.6% of fingerprints unique; AmIUnique (Laperdrix et al., 2016) found about 89.4% unique across 118,000 samples. Frequently asked questions

What is a browser fingerprint?

A browser fingerprint is an identifier built by combining attributes your browser and device expose (user agent, screen, timezone, fonts, canvas/WebGL rendering, and more). Even without cookies, a site can use it to recognise the same browser across visits.

Is this checker safe? Does it upload my data?

No. All reading and computation happen locally in your browser with JavaScript; results are shown only on this page and are never sent to any server or stored.

Why is my fingerprint unique?

Any single attribute (like timezone) is shared by many people, but a dozen attributes combined create a huge value space. Public research shows most browsers have a unique combined fingerprint, so they can be tracked across sites without logging in.

What is a canvas fingerprint?

The browser draws the same text or shapes into a hidden canvas and the pixels are read back. Different GPUs, drivers, system fonts and anti-aliasing produce tiny but stable differences, making canvas one of the most identifying signals.

Does incognito or private mode hide my fingerprint?

Mostly no. Private mode clears cookies and local history, but canvas, WebGL, fonts, timezone and similar signals are still exposed, so private mode alone does not escape fingerprint tracking.

How can I reduce fingerprint tracking?

You can shrink your exposure (use common settings, disable some APIs) or use an anti-detect browser built for multiple accounts - it assigns each browsing profile a separate, controllable and self-consistent fingerprint so accounts stay unlinked.

Can a VPN change my fingerprint?

No. A VPN only changes your exit IP; it does not change canvas, fonts, timezone or user-agent browser signals, so your fingerprint stays the same after switching IP.
